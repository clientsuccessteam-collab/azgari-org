# Letter of Inquiry (LOI) Template

**Purpose.** An LOI is a 1–2 page pitch you send a foundation program officer BEFORE a full proposal. Goal: get the program officer to ask for a full proposal. Most community foundations, corporate foundations, and family foundations use LOIs as a gate — writing a good LOI is the single highest-leverage grant-writing skill.

**How to use this template.**
1. Copy everything between the `---` markers below into a new document per funder.
2. Replace every `{{PLACEHOLDER}}` with the funder-specific or current detail.
3. Keep it to 1 page (single-spaced, 11pt) or at most 2 pages. Program officers will not read past page 2.
4. Attach: the Foundation's fiscal-sponsor confirmation (until 501(c)(3) determination lands), a one-page outcomes snapshot, and a one-page budget.
5. Submit via the funder's LOI portal or email to the program officer directly. Always email, never cold-submit without at least an introductory call or email exchange if possible.

**Key rules.**
- Lead with the problem and the charitable class, not with the Foundation.
- Use THEIR language. If they say "opportunity youth," use "opportunity youth" (it's synonymous with out-of-school youth). Read their last 3 years of grants list on their 990 and mirror their funded-program descriptions.
- Never ask for an amount larger than their median grant. Look up their 990 on Candid or ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer and check typical grant sizes. Ask at or slightly below their median.
- Never promise specific participant outcomes you can't deliver. Use target benchmarks ("target completion rate ≥ 70%") framed as planning numbers, not guarantees.
- Always close with a specific ask and a specific next step.

## Appendix — LOI Quick-Check (before you send)

- [ ] Under 2 pages (ideally 1.5)
- [ ] Funder name, priorities, and a direct quote from their website appear on page 1
- [ ] The problem comes before the Foundation
- [ ] The charitable class is named (out-of-school youth / aging-out foster youth / justice-involved young adults / low-income veterans)
- [ ] The ask amount is at or below their median grant size (verify via their latest 990)
- [ ] The ask is for a specific program component with a specific use
- [ ] Current program ("operating today") is clearly separated from planned expansion ("contingent on funding")
- [ ] At least one outcome target is concrete and trackable (completion rate, placement rate, wage gain)
- [ ] Results disclaimer is present (individual results vary)
- [ ] Next step is specific (conversation, proposal, site visit)
- [ ] No typos in the funder's name or program officer's name
- [ ] Attachments: fiscal-sponsor confirmation letter, 1-page outcomes snapshot, 1-page budget

## Appendix — Common foundation phrases to mirror in your LOI

| Funder term | What it usually means | Use in LOI as |
|---|---|---|
| Opportunity youth | 16–24, not in school or work | "out-of-school youth / opportunity youth" |
| Transition-age youth | 14–24 | "transition-age youth, including aging-out foster youth" |
| Justice-impacted / system-involved | Formerly incarcerated or justice-involved | "justice-involved young adults" |
| Economic mobility | Movement from lower to higher economic status | As the lead benefit of your program |
| Workforce development | Training + placement | "workforce-development program combining training and placement" |
| Capacity building | Grants to strengthen the organization itself | Only use if you're asking for capacity-building dollars |
| General operating support | Unrestricted funds | The ask type you should MOST want |
| Seed / catalytic grant | Smaller first grant to test | Ask for this if you're new to the funder |
| Pooled fund / collaborative | Multiple funders combine | Ask if you can be considered by the collaborative |

## Appendix — Common stats you can cite (verify the source before using)

- ~4.4 million opportunity youth in the U.S. (Opportunity Nation, Measure of America)
- 1 in 5 aging-out foster youth experience homelessness within four years of leaving care (Jim Casey Youth Opportunities Initiative)
- ~60% unemployment among recently-released individuals in year 1 post-release (Brookings Institution; Prison Policy Initiative)
- Veterans are 1.5x more likely than civilians to experience long-term homelessness (HUD AHAR report)
- Median wage gain from sectoral workforce programs: ~14–30% (MDRC evaluations of WorkAdvance, Project QUEST)

Always cite a specific year and source in the actual LOI; refresh stats annually.
